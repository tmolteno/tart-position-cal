\begin{abstract}
	The Transient Array Radio Telescope is an open-source all-sky imaging radio telescope.  For interferometric imaging, the positions of antennas in the array must be known accurately. This paper presents the technique used to calibrate the antenna positions. The technique reports outliers that are then re-measured, and is repeated until the desired accuracy is achieved. It is robust, can be performed with simple equipment and provides accuracy to within $\sim 0.01$ wavelengths, sufficient for interferometric imaging. To determine the global orientation of the array, a further step is required that uses geographical bearings. The results of the TART deployment at the University of Namibia are presented, demonstrating residual errors on the millimeter scale. The calibration algorithm is implemented in the open-source Python package \texttt{tart-position-cal}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Background and Introduction}

%%  TKTK. This does not mention global orientation

The Transient Array Radio Telescope (TART) is an all-sky imaging interferometer that operates in the L1 band (1.575~GHz). The project is open-source~\cite{tart} and to date  TART telescopes have been deployed in Oceania, Africa, Europe, and Asia as a platform to detect radio transients and for the development of new radio imaging algorithms. TART telescopes are often installed as part of a week-long workshop that introduces attendees to radio astronomy, radio imaging, and interferometry, and at the same time assembles and installs a TART radio telescope.

The TART antenna array contains 24 antennas that are typically arranged on five spiral arms, although the layout can vary. For interferometric imaging~\cite{thomsonmoranswenson},

In an interferometric array, an arbitrary point is chosen as the {\em phase-centre} of the array. This phase-centre is the (0,0) point for the antenna coordinate frame. The purpose of this calibration is to accurately estimate the East-North-Up (ENU) coordinates of each antenna with the phase center at the origin.

The position calibration process for the antenna array must determine the co-ordinates of the antennas to within $\approx 5$~mm. The calibration process must involve easy-to-make measurements and be robust to accidental errors in measurement. This paper outlines an iterative calibration process that meets these requirements.

\section{Method}

The calibration process involves measuring two kinds of distances: the \emph{radial distances} between the phase-centre and the centre of each antenna, and the {\em pairwise distances} between all antennas. There are $N$ measurements of radial distance $r_i$, and there are $\frac{1}{2} N (N-1)$ pairwise distances, where $N$ is the number of antennas in the array. For the TART ($N=24$) there are 276 pairwise distances.

As the distance measurements do not constrain the global rotation of the array, an additional constraint is required. It is assumed that the direction between the phase-centre and one of the antennas is known. This direction is the measured geographic bearing between one antenna and a distant landmark with known coordinates.

\begin{figure*}
	\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig/spreadsheet.png}

	\label{fig:spreadsheet}
\end{figure*}

The purpose of the algorithm is to estimate the 24 antenna positions in the $x$--$y$ plane that are most consistent with the measurements.


\subsection{Global Orientation Measurement}

The global orientation of the array cannot be determined from distance measurements alone, as the entire array can be rotated about the phase-centre without changing any of the $r_i$ or $\delta_{ij}$ values. To fix the absolute orientation, the geographic bearing of one antenna from the phase-centre must be measured independently.

This is accomplished by identifying an antenna that has a radial vector that aligns with a distant identifiable landmark visible on satellite imagery (e.g. Google Maps). The geographic coordinates (latitude and longitude) of both the phase-centre and the landmark are recorded, and from these two geographic coordinates, the expected bearing $\theta_k$ of the reference antenna $k$ is computed as an inverse geodesic problem . The tart-position-cal package described in Section~\ref{sec:software}, provides a function to do this.

This $\theta_k$ measurement is supplied to the least-squares procedure as $L_\theta$ in Equation~4, described below.

The global orientation estimate is refined as part of the continuous interferometric calibration that is performed during normal telescope operation~\cite{moltenocalibratoion}.


\subsection{Least-Squares Estimator}

Given any set $\{x_i, y_i\}$ of 24 antenna positions, we can simulate all the measurements $r_i^s$, $\delta_{ij}^s$ and $\theta_k^s$, i.e.,
\begin{align*}
	r^s_i         & =  \sqrt{x_i^2 + y_i^2}                \\
	\delta^s_{ij} & = \sqrt{(x_i - x_j)^2 + (y_i - y_j)^2} \\
	\theta^s_k    & = \arctan2\!\left(x_k, y_k\right).
\end{align*}
These simulated measurements will be close to the recorded measurements if the $\{x_i, y_i\}$ are close to the true values.

The differences between the simulated measurements and the actual measurements are called {\em residuals}. The set of positions that minimizes the squared residuals $L(\{x_i, y_i\})$, is a good estimate of the true antenna positions $\{x_i, y_i\}$. We form least-squares estimators from the sum of the radius, difference, and $\theta$ residuals squared:
\begin{align*}
	L_r(\{x_i, y_i\})      & = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left( r_i - r^s_i \right)^2              \\
	L_{ij}(\{x_i, y_i\})   & = \sum_{i<j} \left( \delta_{ij} -  \delta^s_{ij} \right)^2 \\
	L_\theta(\{x_i, y_i\}) & = \left( \theta_k - \theta^s_k \right)^2.
\end{align*}

As we wish to minimize each of these estimators, we form a combined estimator $L(\{x_i, y_i\})$ from their weighted sum:
\begin{align}
	L(\{x_i, y_i\}) = w_r\, L_r(\{x_i, y_i\}) + L_{ij}(\{x_i, y_i\}) + \lambda\, L_\theta(\{x_i, y_i\}) + \gamma\, L_\chi
\end{align}
Here $w_r$ up-weights the radial residuals (default $w_r = 10$, reflecting that the single-ended radial distances are measured more reliably than the pairwise ones), $\lambda \sim 100$ constrains the global rotation, and $\gamma \sim 10^6$ applies the one-sided chirality penalty $L_\chi$ that breaks the reflection degeneracy (see \S\ref{sec:symmetries}). The pairwise sum $L_{ij}$ runs over the $\tfrac{1}{2}N(N-1)$ unique antenna pairs (276 for $N=24$), counting each measured distance once. When no chirality constraint is requested, $L_\chi = 0$.

\subsection{Algorithm}

The algorithm starts with a set of \emph{initial estimates} of $x_i, y_i$, the coordinates of the $i$th antenna. These are obtained from the telescope's nominal design positions, queried from the telescope API or loaded from a JSON configuration file. From these, the simulated measurements $r^s_i = \sqrt{x_i^2 + y_i^2}$, and $\delta^s_{ij} = \sqrt{(x_i - x_j)^2 + (y_i - y_j)^2}$ are calculated. The least squares estimator $L(x,y)$ is then minimized (as a function of $x_i, y_i$) using the trust-region reflective (TRF) least-squares algorithm (\texttt{scipy.optimize.least\_squares}) in the SciPy library~\cite{scipy}, to find the set of antenna positions that produce simulated measurements as close as possible to the actual measurements. The algorithm is implemented in the open-source Python package \texttt{tart-position-cal}~\cite{moltento2026tartpositioncal}.  The geographic bearing of the reference antenna is obtained from the \texttt{compute\_bearing()} function, which solves the inverse geodesic problem on the WGS84 ellipsoid:

\begin{verbatim}
from tart_position_cal import compute_bearing
# UNAM phase-centre to distant hill
bearing = compute_bearing(-22.612053, 17.056784,
                          -22.553822, 17.077290)
# bearing = 18.1125...
\end{verbatim}

The full calibration is then invoked as:

\begin{verbatim}
tart-position-cal \
    --measurements antenna_measurements.ods \
    --positions antenna_positions.json \
    --output calibrated_antenna_positions.json \
    --rot-index 3 \
    --rot-degrees 18.1125 \
    --chirality-index 10 \
    --chirality-sign negative
\end{verbatim}

This results in a list of residuals.

\subsection{Residuals and Iterative Refinement}

If one or more of the residuals are large, it usually indicates that the associated measurement is not consistent with the other measurements. These measurements that result in large residuals are repeated and the corresponding measurements are repeated. The algorithm is then re-run with the corrected measurements to achieve a new set of residuals. This iterative process continues until all residuals fall below an acceptable threshold. A flow-chart of this is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:flowchart}.

\subsection{Symmetries and Global Frame Determination}
\label{sec:symmetries}

The distance measurements $r_i$ and $\delta_{ij}$ alone do not fully determine the antenna positions. The problem possesses three symmetries that must each be resolved by an additional constraint:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item \textbf{Translation.} Shifting all antennas by a common vector leaves all pairwise distances unchanged. This is resolved by fixing the phase-centre at $(0,0)$, since all radial distances $r_i$ are measured relative to this point.
	\item \textbf{Rotation.} Rotating the entire array about the phase-centre preserves all $r_i$ and $\delta_{ij}$. This is resolved by the geographic bearing constraint $\theta_k$ (Equation~4), which pins one reference antenna to a known direction.
	\item \textbf{Reflection (chirality).} Reflecting the array across any line through the origin preserves all distances. The single bearing constraint $\theta_k$ does \emph{not} eliminate this degeneracy: a reflection across the line at bearing $\theta_k$ maps the reference antenna to the same bearing, producing an identical value of $L(x,y)$. The true configuration and its mirror image are therefore degenerate global minima of the unconstrained problem.
\end{enumerate}

To break the reflection degeneracy, the \texttt{tart-position-cal} package provides a \emph{chirality constraint}, invoked with the \texttt{--chirality-index} flag. A second antenna $c$, not collinear with the reference antenna $k$, is chosen. The cross product $\mathbf{p}_k \times \mathbf{p}_c = x_k y_c - y_k x_c$ encodes the chirality (handedness) of the configuration. The correct sign $s = \pm 1$ is specified explicitly by the operator via the \texttt{--chirality-sign} CLI flag (\texttt{positive} $= +1$, \texttt{negative} $= -1$), determined from an on-site observation of the as-built array rather than inferred from the initial position estimates. A one-sided penalty is applied if the optimizer attempts to flip that sign. Normalized by the measured radial distances $r_k r_c$, the penalty is dimensionless and uses a default weight of $10^6$, acting as an effective hard constraint. In IRLS mode, the chirality residual is excluded from the Tukey/Huber reweighting so that it is never relaxed by the robust weighting scheme.

With the phase-centre, bearing constraint, and chirality constraint all active, the six degrees of freedom of the rigid transformation group in the plane are fully eliminated, and the global antenna positions are uniquely determined.

\paragraph{Choosing the chirality antenna and sign.}
The chirality antenna $c$ should lie on a different spiral arm from the reference antenna $k$, as close to a right angle from the reference bearing as possible.  Antennas near the reference bearing line (or its 180$^\circ$ opposite) produce a fragile, easily-flipped signal and should be avoided.

To determine the sign to pass to \texttt{--chirality-sign}, stand at the phase centre and face the reference antenna $k$.  Extend an imaginary line through $k$ --- this is the reference bearing line.  If the chirality antenna $c$ is to your \textbf{left}, the chirality is \textbf{positive}; if to your \textbf{right}, it is \textbf{negative}.  The sign can be verified after calibration by computing $x_k y_c - y_k x_c$ from the output positions: a positive value corresponds to \texttt{positive}, a negative to \texttt{negative}.

\section{The \texttt{tart-position-cal} Python Package}
\label{sec:software}

The calibration algorithm is implemented in the open-source Python package \texttt{tart-position-cal}~\cite{moltento2026tartpositioncal}, available under the GPL-3.0 license. The package provides both a command-line interface (CLI) and a programmatic API, and is installable via \texttt{pip} from the Python Package Index.

\subsection{Command-Line Interface}

The CLI is invoked as \texttt{tart-position-cal} and accepts the following principal arguments:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \texttt{--measurements}: Path to an ODS spreadsheet (in the format shown in Figure~\ref{fig:spreadsheet}) containing the radial and pairwise distance measurements.
	\item \texttt{--positions}: Path to a JSON file with initial antenna position estimates in metres, structured as \texttt{\{"antenna\_positions": [[x, y, z], \ldots]\}}.
	\item \texttt{--api}: Alternatively, a TART telescope API base URL from which initial positions can be fetched directly (e.g.\ \texttt{https://api.elec.ac.nz/tart/na-unam}).
	\item \texttt{--output}: Path for the calibrated antenna positions JSON output file.
	\item \texttt{--rot-index} and \texttt{--rot-degrees}: Specify the reference antenna and its geographic bearing for the global rotation constraint.
	\item \texttt{--chirality-index}: Antenna index used to break the reflection (chirality) degeneracy.  The sign of $\mathbf{p}_k \times \mathbf{p}_c$ from the initial positions is enforced as a soft constraint (see \S\ref{sec:symmetries}).  Requires \texttt{--rot-index}.
	\item \texttt{--irls}: Enable Iteratively Reweighted Least Squares for robust calibration (see \S\ref{sec:irls}).
	\item \texttt{--irls-weight-fn}: Weight function for IRLS: \texttt{tukey} (Tukey's bisquare, default) or \texttt{huber}.
	\item \texttt{--irls-max-iter}: Maximum IRLS outer iterations (default 10).
	\item \texttt{--max-iter}: Maximum number of optimizer iterations (default 500).
	\item \texttt{--max-position-error}: Half-width of the search bounds around each initial coordinate (default 4200~mm).
	\item \texttt{--no-plots}: Suppress generation of diagnostic plots.
	\item \texttt{--plot-dir}: Directory for diagnostic plots (default: current directory).
	\item \texttt{--title}: Optional prefix prepended to all output file names (e.g.\ \texttt{na-unam}).
\end{itemize}

The program outputs a JSON file compatible with the TART telescope API, together with three diagnostic plots: the initial and calibrated positions, the displacement vectors from initial to calibrated positions, and a histogram of the pairwise distance residuals.

\subsection{Programmatic API}

The core calibration logic resides in the \texttt{tart\_position\_cal.calibrate} module. The principal functions are:

\begin{itemize}
	\item \texttt{calibrate()}: Runs the standard least-squares optimization using the trust-region reflective (TRF) algorithm (\texttt{scipy.optimize.least\_squares}). Returns a SciPy \texttt{OptimizeResult} object.
	\item \texttt{calibrate\_irls()}: Runs the IRLS variant (see \S\ref{sec:irls}) using Trust Region Reflective least squares as the inner solver.
	\item \texttt{load\_measurements()}: Reads radial and pairwise distance measurements from an ODS spreadsheet via \texttt{pandas} and \texttt{odfpy}.
	\item \texttt{load\_initial\_positions()} / \texttt{fetch\_initial\_positions()}: Obtain initial antenna position estimates from a JSON file or a TART API endpoint, respectively.
	\item \texttt{result\_to\_json()}: Converts the optimizer output to the telescope's API-compatible JSON format.
	\item \texttt{compute\_bearing()}: Computes the geographic bearing (degrees east of north) from the phase-centre to a distant landmark by solving the inverse geodesic problem on the WGS84 ellipsoid via the PROJ library~\cite{proj}. This is the value to pass to \texttt{--rot-degrees}.
\end{itemize}

Dependencies are restricted to widely available scientific Python libraries: NumPy~\cite{numpy}, SciPy~\cite{scipy}, pandas~\cite{pandas}, Matplotlib~\cite{matplotlib}, Requests, and PROJ (pyproj)~\cite{proj}. The package is tested with Python 3.11 and later.

\section{Results: UNAM Deployment}

The TART telescope at the University of Namibia (UNAM) in Windhoek is located at latitude $-22.6121^\circ$, longitude $17.0568^\circ$. The 24 antennas are arranged on five spiral arms, extending to a maximum radius of approximately 1.7~m from the phase-centre.

\subsection{Initial Conditions}

The initial position estimates were obtained from the telescope's configuration file, with coordinates in meters converted to millimeters for the optimization. The initial least-squares cost $L(x,y)$ was $3.5 \times 10^7$, indicating substantial disagreement between the nominal positions and the measured distances.

\subsection{Global Orientation}

The global orientation was determined using antenna~3 (index 3 in the array numbering). A distant hill at coordinates $(-22.553822^\circ, 17.077290^\circ)$ was identified as a landmark visible along the antenna arm. The computed bearing from the telescope to the hill was $\beta = 18.1125^\circ$. This angle was used as the rotation constraint $L_\theta$ in the least-squares minimization, with the reference antenna constrained to this bearing.

\subsection{Calibration Results}

\subsection{Iteratively Reweighted Least Squares}
\label{sec:irls}

When survey measurements contain outliers---erroneous distance readings from misaligned tape measures or transcription errors---ordinary least squares can be skewed by those bad measurements. To address this, the \texttt{tart-position-cal} package includes an Iteratively Reweighted Least Squares (IRLS) option, invoked with the \texttt{--irls} flag. IRLS solves a series of weighted least-squares problems, recomputing per-measurement weights after each iteration to down-weight outliers. Two weight functions are available: Tukey's bisquare (default), which zeros out clear outliers entirely, and Huber, which caps the influence of outliers with a saturating linear penalty.

Applying IRLS with Tukey weights to the UNAM data reduced the inter-antenna residual statistics: median absolute deviation from 0.63~mm to 0.49~mm, standard deviation from 1.11~mm to 1.11~mm, 50th percentile from 0.59~mm to 0.49~mm, and 90th percentile from 1.65~mm to 1.44~mm. The outer loop converged in 4 iterations. Note that while these robust statistics improve, the unweighted objective $L(x,y)$ rises from $4.35\times 10^2$ to $5.40\times 10^2$, because the outliers that dominate it are precisely those that IRLS down-weights. Figure~\ref{fig:irls_residuals} shows the residual histogram for the IRLS calibration. Compared to the manual iterative re-measurement workflow, IRLS provides a more robust solution, though manual verification of flagged outlier pairs is still required, fewer measurements need to be redone.

\section{Conclusions}

The least-squares position calibration method presented here provides a practical and accurate means of determining antenna positions for the TART radio telescope. The technique requires only a tape measure and a means of recording geographic coordinates for one reference bearing, making it suitable for workshop-based telescope deployments.

Results from the UNAM deployment demonstrate that the method converges reliably from initial position errors on the order of tens of millimeters to a solution with millimeter-scale residuals. The median absolute deviation of 0.63~mm and 90th percentile of 1.65~mm correspond to approximately $0.003\lambda$ and $0.009\lambda$ at the L1 frequency, well within the requirement for interferometric imaging.

The iterative nature of the method, with outlier flagging and re-measurement, makes it robust to the inevitable measurement errors that occur during manual surveying. The calibrated positions can be uploaded directly to the telescope API and used for imaging without further adjustment.

\section{Future Work}

The TART telescope operates in the L1 band at 1.575~GHz, which is shared with GNSS signals. The raw telescope data streams contain embedded GNSS satellite signals at known frequencies. Post-processing these signals via software-defined radio techniques could yield direct time-difference-of-arrival (TDOA) measurements between antenna pairs, providing an independent and fully automated means of estimating antenna positions. Since GNSS satellite ephemerides are precisely known, this approach could in principle determine absolute antenna positions without any manual surveying. This is an area of active research. The main challenge lies in the low signal-to-noise ratio of the GNSS signals in the TART's broad-beam antennas and the short baselines ($<$\,2~m), which produce TDOA values on the order of nanoseconds.
